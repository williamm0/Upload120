How it works

Upload120 rewrites two metadata fields inside an MP4 - mvhd (movie header) and mdhd (media header) - so the upload pipeline reads the timeline as if the source were standard 30 fps. Your real frames stay untouched, only the timescale and duration values are divided by your chosen multiplier.

For best results: source video should be a fixed-rate MP4 at 60 or 120 fps, and the patched file must be uploaded from a computer. Phone uploads re-encode and undo the trick.

Why this also gives better quality

The neat side-effect: your video usually looks sharper after this method, not just smoother. Three things stack up.

1. Higher per-frame bitrate. TikTok's encoder picks a bitrate budget per second of apparent video. A "30 fps × 4 s" clip gets the standard 30-fps budget, but it's distributed across 4× as many real frames. Effective per-frame bitrate goes up - less blocking, less banding, more detail preserved.

2. Softer compression path. TikTok routes high-FPS sources through an aggressive temporal-smoothing pipeline that drops, blends, or hard-rounds frames. With the metadata reading as 30 fps, the file skips that pipeline. The original frames pass through closer to source.

3. Reduced motion-quantisation. Encoders allocate fewer bits to predicted frames in fast motion. Apparent slow motion (lower fps) means motion vectors look "calmer" - quantisation stays gentle, edges stay clean.

Net result: real 60/120 fps motion plus a noticeably crisper image versus a normal upload. The bigger the source frame rate, the bigger the win.

Why a desktop upload

The TikTok mobile app forces a stricter re-encode pipeline that always lands at 30 fps regardless of metadata. The web upload path is more permissive and reads the rewritten timescale, which is what lets your original 60/120 fps frames pass through to playback. Upload120 talks to that same web pipeline directly - the file goes in through the desktop path even though you posted from this app.
